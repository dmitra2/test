HISTORY OF STONE PAPER SCISSOR

Origins in China

➤

The game's earliest known mention can be traced back to the Ming dynasty, documented in the book "Wuzazu" authored by Xie Zhaozhe, a prominent writer of the era, whose activity centered around the 1600s. Within "Wuzazu," Xie Zhaozhe attributes the game to the Han dynasty, a period extending from 206 BCE to 220 CE, suggesting a venerable lineage for the game.

Xie Zhaozhe identifies the game by the name "shoushiling" in "Wuzazu," providing an initial insight into its historical appellation. Moreover, Li Rihua's "Note of Liuyanzhai" further elucidates the game's nomenclature, introducing alternative names such as "huozhitou" and "huaquan" alongside "shoushiling." This additional detail offers a richer understanding of the linguistic diversity surrounding the game across different historical sources.

These literary references not only shed light on the game's antiquity but also hint at its cultural significance during various periods in Chinese history. By delving into the terminology used to describe the game, scholars can glean insights into its evolution and adaptation across different regions and eras within China.

Introduction to Japan

➤

The game, originally from China, found its way to Japan where it became known as sansukumi-ken, or "fist games of the three who are afraid of one another." The earliest version in Japan was mushi-ken, believed to have been directly imported from China. In mushi-ken, gestures representing a frog, a slug, and a snake determine the outcome, with the frog triumphing over the slug, the slug over the snake, and the snake over the frog. Although the Chinese and Japanese versions initially differed in the animals represented due to a character confusion, mushi-ken laid the groundwork for later variations.

The most popular iteration in Japan, kitsune-ken, featured a fox defeating the village head, the village head defeating the hunter, and the hunter defeating the fox. Kitsune-ken distinguished itself from other versions by requiring gestures with both hands.

Today, the widely recognized version of sansukumi-ken is jan-ken, a variation introduced from China in the 17th century. Jan-ken utilizes the rock, paper, and scissors signs, which directly influenced the modern rendition of rock paper scissors. Hand-games representing these conflicting elements have been prevalent since the late 19th century, bridging the Edo and Meiji periods.

Spread Beyond East Asia

➤

By the early 20th century, the game of rock paper scissors transcended its origins in East Asia, proliferating globally, especially due to increased Japanese interactions with the Western world. Its English name derives from a translation of the Japanese hand gestures for rock, paper, and scissors. However, in other parts of East Asia, such as China, the open-palm gesture signifies "cloth" instead of "paper." Notably, the distinct shape of the scissors used in the game is also influenced by the Japanese style.

In various publications around the world during the early 20th century, rock paper scissors was depicted under different names and contexts. For instance, a 1921 article in the Sydney Morning Herald referred to it as "stone, scissors, and paper," labeling it as a "Teutonic method of drawing lots" encountered while traveling in Europe. Another article from the same year in the Washington Herald described it as a form of "Chinese gambling." Similarly, in Britain in 1924, it was described in a letter to The Times as a hand game possibly of Mediterranean origin called "zhot," later identified as the Japanese game Jan-ken-pon. These references highlight the game's growing international recognition and its various cultural interpretations.

Rock paper scissors also found its way into literature, further indicating its familiarity in different parts of the world. For instance, in Hildegard G. Frey's novels in the Campfire Girls series from the early 20th century, the game is mentioned without explanation, suggesting its widespread understanding among American readers. Furthermore, in 1927, a children's magazine in France, La Vie au patronage, provided a detailed description of the game, referring to it as a "jeu japonais" ("Japanese game"). Its French name, "Chi-fou-mi," is rooted in Old Japanese words for "one, two, three" ("hi, fu, mi"). These instances underscore the global diffusion of rock paper scissors and its incorporation into mainstream culture across continents.
